Genuine Reasoning or Clever Mimicry? Evidence for Algorithmic Strategies in Language Models

Reasoning models are one of the most exciting frontiers in AI today. These are language models trained to solve problems using human-like chains of thought. Examples include OpenAI's "O" series, DeepSeek's R1, and Anthropic's Claude "thinking" models. A defining feature of these models is their ability to generate detailed chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning during inference‚Äîwithout being explicitly prompted to do so by the user. This emergent behavior has been shown to significantly boost performance in tasks like mathematics and code generation.

But while their outputs can appear strikingly human-like, important questions remain:

  • What is the model actually doing during its reasoning step? Is the reasoning logically valid, or is it superficial?
  • Are the reasoning traces faithful? Do they reflect the true internal computation of the model, or are they merely plausible-sounding justifications post hoc?

These questions go beyond benchmark scores and touch on the honesty and interpretability of reasoning models. If reasoning traces are to serve as a window into the model's internal decision-making, we need to be confident that what we're seeing reflects what the model is actually doing.

This concern was brought into sharp focus by a recent paper from Apple provocatively titled, "The Illusion of Thinking: Understanding the Strengths and Limitations of Reasoning Models via the Lens of Problem Complexity." The paper challenges the validity of reasoning traces, suggesting that, particularly as task complexity increases, these models may be "reasoning" more in appearance than in substance.

There's also been fascinating work in mechanistic interpretability aimed at unpacking how these reasoning behaviors emerge. One paper that especially inspired this post is "The Geometry of Self-Verification in a Task-Specific Reasoning Model" by researchers at Harvard. It investigates internal mechanisms that allow reasoning models to self-verify‚Äîi.e., check whether their own answers are correct.

In this post, I analyze a reasoning model trained on the Countdown task and find that it learns and employs optimal strategies in its reasoning traces. I empirically validate this by dissecting the model's internal activations, focusing on the residual stream, to reveal how these strategies are embedded within the model's computation.

Rather than evaluating the accuracy of a model's final answer, I focus on the process behind it. How does the model reason its way to a solution? Are its reasoning traces consistent with human problem-solving strategies? And perhaps most importantly: are they honest? Or is the model simply stringing together fluent, logical-sounding English that masks a more brittle, shortcut-based underlying process?

Technical Details

Training Problem: Countdown

The Countdown game itself is simple in concept but surprisingly difficult for non-reasoning models to solve. The model is given a target number and a set of three or four candidate numbers. Using only basic arithmetic operations (+, ‚àí, √ó, √∑), the goal is to combine all the numbers to exactly reach the target. Below is an example of a problem the model was trained on along with a potential solution.

Target: 20 Candidates: [31, 91, 44, 36]
Solution: 36 + 31 + 44 - 91

I chose this task for two main reasons:

  • Inspired by prior work: This setup builds on themes from The Geometry of Self-Verification paper, which studied internal mechanisms in reasoning models. Like that paper, I was interested in whether the model learns to verify or follow consistent strategies.
  • Numerical structure enables clear analysis: Unlike open-ended tasks involving natural language or commonsense reasoning, Countdown is highly structured and numerical. This makes it easier to evaluate whether the model is genuinely reasoning toward a solution‚Äîor simply producing plausible-sounding outputs. There's less ambiguity about what the "correct" steps are, which allows us to cleanly test whether the model is following an optimal or interpretable strategy.

Model & Training Setup

I built the reasoning model using Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct as the foundation. The model underwent post-training using GRPO (Group Relative Policy Optimization)‚Äîthe reinforcement learning approach popularized by DeepSeek. Remarkably, meaningful reasoning behaviors emerged after just 450 training steps on a Countdown dataset.

The training process was guided by two complementary reward functions that together shaped the model's reasoning behavior:

  1. Formatting Reward
    Enforces structured output by requiring every response to include explicit reasoning steps within <think>...</think> tags, followed by a final solution within <answer>...</answer> tags. This ensures the model develops the habit of "thinking out loud."
  2. Task Accuracy Reward
    Validates correctness by evaluating whether the mathematical expression in the <answer> tags produces the target number when computed. This grounds the reasoning in actual problem-solving success.

Together, these rewards create a training environment where the model must not only arrive at correct answers but also articulate a coherent reasoning process‚Äîsetting the stage for our investigation into what the model is actually doing internally.

Prior to training, the base model only managed to solve 2.5% of the problems in the test set. After training the model was able to solve 47% of the problems in the test set. While this might seem low overall solve rate, with more training I expect the accuracy to continue improving. I was limited due to my compute budget.

For the rest of the post, I will remove the <think> and <answer> tags from most of the model output examples I show for readability.

To begin unpacking how our model "thinks," we start with the most direct signal available: its reasoning traces. In the Countdown task, these traces reflect the model's step-by-step attempts to reach the target number using the provided candidate numbers and basic arithmetic operations. The contrast between the base model and the reasoning model is striking. The base model's outputs are incomplete and lack systematic exploration‚Äîin Example 1, it simply acknowledges the task without attempting any calculations, and in Example 2, it begins to reason but trails off without reaching a conclusion. Both examples also fail to provide a final answer. The reasoning model, by contrast, demonstrates a methodical approach: it systematically tests different combinations, evaluates each result against the target, and continues iterating until it finds a solution. This structured problem-solving behavior emerged directly from the GRPO training process.

At first glance, this looks like genuine reasoning. The output is structured, coherent, and often mirrors the kind of step-by-step approach a human might use. To a casual observer, this is compelling evidence that the model is engaging in logical thought.

However, I want to go deeper. I'm not just interested in whether the reasoning looks good‚ÄîI want to understand what's actually happening under the hood. Is the model following a learned, interpretable strategy? Or is it producing fluent language that appears rational but doesn't reflect the model's internal computation? Is there any intentional structure to the reasoning, or is it just well-formed output optimized to sound plausible?

These are the core questions that guide the rest of this investigation.

Beyond Surface Reasoning: Is There a Strategy?

In the previous section, we saw that the model's reasoning traces are coherent and structured. But coherent output alone isn't enough‚Äîwe want to know what kind of computation the model is performing internally. Is it actually following an algorithmic strategy? Or is it just trying out plausible arithmetic operations at random until it lands on a correct answer?

To probe this, we turn our attention to how the model iterates toward its solution.

Answer Iteration

The key question here is: Is there any algorithmic structure behind the model's reasoning steps, or is it effectively just sampling valid combinations until it gets lucky?

At a glance, it's already clear that the model is doing something more sophisticated than random guessing. We can reason about this probabilistically by considering the number of valid expression combinations in the Countdown problem.

For a given set of N candidate numbers, the number of unique, valid expressions that can be formed is:

Total combinations = 2 × 4N − 1 × N!

Derivation üìù

With N numbers, there are N ! possible orderings. For each arrangement, we need N − 1 operators, and each operator can be one of four choices (+, ‚àí, √ó, √∑), resulting in 4N − 1 combinations. The factor of 2 comes from the fact that the very first number in the sequence can be either added or subtracted.

For example:

  • When N=4, there are 3,072 possible expressions.
  • When N=3, there are 192 possible expressions.

Yet, our model typically solves these problems in just 5 steps on average‚Äîfar fewer than what random search would require.

If that's not convincing enough, we can turn to a more visual comparison.

Figure 1 shows the intermediate reasoning values produced at each step for two different approaches: a random reasoning sequence and the model-generated reasoning sequence from Example 1. The contrast is striking. The model's approach, by contrast, remains much more constrained and exhibits a notably different pattern. Despite a notable jump in the penultimate step, the model demonstrates clear signs of iteratively converging toward the target solution, suggesting a more systematic and controlled reasoning process.

These comparisons provide strong evidence that the model has learned a more sophisticated strategy than brute-force search. It's not just sampling expressions at random‚Äîit seems to be executing a more directed process.

But that still leaves us with deeper questions: What is that strategy? Can we find evidence for it inside the model itself‚Äîwithin its activations or internal representations? That's what we'll explore next.

Signs of an Algorithm

The Largest Number First: A Strategic Pattern

One of the first patterns I noticed while analyzing reasoning traces was surprisingly consistent: the model almost always begins by selecting the largest number in the candidate set. This behavior holds true across the examples we discussed earlier.

Examples of Model's First Reasoning Steps

Example 1:We need to use the numbers 85, 54, 32, and 61 with basic arithmetic operations to get 46.

First step: 85 - 54 - 32 + 61 = 89 (too high)

Example 2: We need to use the numbers 9, 80, and 5 with basic arithmetic operations to make 84.

First step: 80 - 9 - 5 = 66 (too low)

Example 3: We need to use the numbers 94, 44, and 81 with basic arithmetic operations to get 57.

First step: 94 - 44 - 81 = -27 (too low)

Example 4: We need to use the numbers 24, 71, 92, and 3 with basic arithmetic operations to make 91.

First step: 92 - 71 + 24 + 3 = 44 (too low)

Example 5: We need to use the numbers 33, 72, and 44 with basic arithmetic operations to get 96.

First step: 72 - 33 + 44 = 125 (too high)

Notice: In every case, the model begins with the largest candidate number (highlighted in yellow).

To quantify this, I analyzed 120 reasoning traces and found that over 95% of them began by choosing the largest number as the first operand. If the selection were random, this would only occur ~29% of the time (since the dataset contains examples of both 3 and 4 candidate numbers).

This suggests the model is making a deliberate, learned decision to always start with the largest number. So the next natural questions are: Why would the model make such a strategic decision? What part of the model is responsible for this behavior? Can we pinpoint the internal mechanism that identifies and selects the largest number?

The Mathematical Rationale Behind the Strategy

Before diving into the model's behavior, it's important to clarify one subtle but crucial point:

In the Countdown arithmetic game, starting an expression with the largest number is functionally equivalent to using the largest number with the addition operator. Since the first number in an expression isn't paired with an operator, placing the largest number first inherently means it's being added to the rest of the expression. This makes "starting with the largest number" and "adding the largest number" interchangeable in this context.

With that in mind, it might still seem unintuitive‚Äîwhy would always starting with the largest number be a smart strategy? When we consider the structure of the Countdown dataset, however, the rationale becomes clearer.

To understand this behavior, I ran a brute-force analysis. For each problem in the dataset, I generated all valid arithmetic expressions using the provided numbers, then filtered for those that evaluated exactly to the target. Among these correct solutions, I measured how often the largest number was added‚Äîeither by being used first or explicitly in an addition.

The result was striking: the largest number was added in correct expressions 73% of the time.

This strongly suggests that the model is employing a simple strategy that is effective in the Countdown game. It has learned that adding the largest number gives it a higher chance of success vs other operations‚Äîso it adopts that strategy consistently.

Inside the Model: Uncovering the Sorting Mechanism

We have observed what the model is doing, and we have a good idea of why it's doing it. The final question is: how is the model doing it? What internal mehcanims is responsible for this particular strategy?

To answer this, we need to dive into the model's internal computations‚Äîspecifically, into how it decides which token to predict next.

Language models are trained as next-token predictors. At each step, they compute a probability distribution over all possible tokens by applying a softmax to the logits‚Äîthe raw scores output by the model before normalization. The token with the highest logit is the one the model predicts.

Note üìù

In practice, sampling methods like temperature or top-p can introduce randomness so the top logit isn't always selected, but for my analysis I focus on the deterministic case where the highest logit token is chosen.

We can examine this process by looking at the logit distribution for the very first digit that the model outputs in its initial reasoning step. Consider this example:

However, this analysis only reveals what the model chose‚Äînot the internal computational process that led to this decision.

The Residual Stream

To understand how a model arrives at a decision, we need to trace its internal computation. In transformer models, this often means looking at the residual stream‚Äîa running sum of outputs from each layer, including the original input embeddings. Every attention head and MLP reads from and writes to this stream, which effectively acts as the model's working memory.

Figure 2, which I created, compares two ways of visualizing this architecture. On the left is the conventional depiction, where the residual stream loops around each block. This layout mirrors the actual implementation, where the stream passes information forward without performing any computation itself. On the right is an alternative layout I designed to emphasize information flow. By placing the residual stream at the center and showing each computational block as plugging into it, the diagram reframes the residual stream as the core structure‚Äîthe evolving workspace to which all computations contribute.

Because of its additive structure, the residual stream is especially useful for interpretation. It lets us isolate the impact of each layer on the model's predictions‚Äîrevealing, for example, where specific behaviors (like selecting the largest number) start to take shape.

Figure 2: Two perspectives on transformer architecture - traditional view (left) vs. residual stream-centered view (right). (Diagram by me)

To analyze this, we use a method called the logit lens. Rather than just examining the final output, we project the residual stream at each layer through the model's unembedding matrix. This shows us what the model would have predicted at that point in its computation.

Using the logit lens, we can track how the logit difference between the largest number and other candidates evolves across layers‚Äîgiving us a window into when and where the model begins to "decide."

Visualizing the Internal Model Mechanisms

Using Example 1 from earlier (target: 20, candidates = [31, 91, 44, 36]), a straightforward way to identify which layers are responsible for finding the largest number is to compare the logits for the token 9 (from 91) against those for 3 and 4 (from 31, 36, and 44), just as we did in the previous section.

In Figure 3, I plot the logit difference between 9 and the others across all 37 layers of Qwen2.5-VL-3B-Instruct. Surprisingly, there's almost no distinction in logits for the first 27 layers. Then, around layer 33, we see a sharp spike in the logit difference‚Äîindicating a sudden emergence of preference for the largest number. This strongly suggests that some computation in layers 30‚Äì33, possibly within an attention or MLP block, is responsible for identifying the maximum value. This delayed emergence makes sense: number comparison is a relatively high-level task that likely requires earlier aggregation and contextualization steps.

Figure 3: Logit differences across layers showing emergence of sorting behavior

We can take this a step further. Instead of comparing entire layers, we can track the logit differences before and after each sub-layer, i.e., attention and MLP blocks. Figure 4 shows the result of this finer-grained analysis‚Äîand it's striking. The most significant jump occurs after Attention Block 32, pointing to this specific attention block as the key mechanism behind the model's "largest number" decision.

Figure 4: Logit differences across each computation block within the model

But attention blocks aren't monolithic‚Äîthey consist of multiple heads, each operating independently. So, we drilled down further to inspect which individual heads are contributing most to the logit difference. The result? The most influential are Head 7 in layers 32, 33, and 31 (in that order), with Head 7 in layer 32 being the most dominant‚Äîaligning perfectly with the spike we observed in Figure 4.

When we inspect the attention patterns of this head, we see something remarkable: it shifts attention strongly toward the token corresponding to the largest number and channels that information toward the prediction slot. In essence, it's performing a max operation.

Even more surprisingly, this head does more than just identify the largest number. It also attends‚Äîthough less strongly‚Äîto the second- and third-largest numbers in order. This behavior closely resembles numerical sorting: the head is actively prioritizing inputs by magnitude!

This is a remarkable finding: we've uncovered a sorting attention head. Through training alone‚Äîwithout any explicit supervision‚Äîthe model has learned that identifying the largest number is an optimal strategy for this task, and it has encoded that strategy in a specialized, interpretable attention head.

Conclusion

By analyzing reasoning traces, intermediate logits, and internal attention patterns, we've uncovered clear evidence that the model isn't just "sounding smart"‚Äîit has learned an interpretable, algorithmic process to solve Countdown problems. It identifies and prioritizes the largest number, because doing so maximizes its chances of success.

Of course, this sorting mechanism is just one part of the model's overall problem-solving approach‚Äîthere are undoubtedly other computational processes at work that contribute to its reasoning abilities. But this particular insight provides a concrete example of algorithmic behavior emerging from training.

The implications are significant for our understanding of reasoning models. Rather than being mere pattern-matching systems that produce plausible-sounding reasoning traces, these models can learn genuine strategies that reflect optimal problem-solving approaches. Moreover, the reasoning traces we observe aren't just post-hoc justifications‚Äîthey appear to be faithful representations of the model's internal computation.

This work opens up exciting avenues for future research. Can we find similar algorithmic structures in other reasoning tasks? How do these strategies emerge during training? And most importantly, can we leverage these insights to build more interpretable and trustworthy AI systems?
